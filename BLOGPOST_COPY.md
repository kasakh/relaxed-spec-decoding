# DIVERSED: Relaxed Speculative Decoding via Dynamic Ensemble Verification

Speculative decoding is one of the most practical ideas for accelerating large language model inference: a small draft model proposes several tokens, and a larger target model verifies them in parallel. In principle, this can substantially reduce latency. In practice, however, the speedup is often bottlenecked by the verifier itself. Standard speculative decoding is rigid: it accepts draft tokens only when doing so preserves the exact target distribution. That exactness is elegant, but it also means many plausible and task-compatible draft tokens get rejected.

Our paper introduces **DIVERSED** (**D**ynam**I**c **VE**rification **RE**laxed **Sp**eculative **E**coding), a relaxed verification framework that improves acceptance without sacrificing output quality. The main idea is to verify draft tokens against a learned mixture of the target and draft distributions rather than the target alone. Crucially, the mixture weight is not fixed. It is predicted dynamically from the hidden states of both models, allowing the verifier to be strict when correctness is fragile and more permissive when the draft token is likely safe. We train this verifier using sequence-level reinforcement learning with a task reward and an acceptance-oriented regularizer.

The paper also provides a theoretical lens on relaxed verification. We show that a simple **static ensemble** verifier already traces the Pareto-optimal trade-off between acceptance and distributional bias. This gives a strong training-free baseline and clarifies the role of verification relaxation. DIVERSED builds on this by replacing the fixed mixture weight with a context-dependent one, letting the system adapt token by token. We also derive an exact step-dependent characterization of the expected accepted length, removing simplifying assumptions from prior analyses.

Empirically, DIVERSED improves acceptance across reasoning, summarization, and code-generation benchmarks. On the Llama-3.1-8B / Llama-3.2-1B pair, acceptance on CNNDM rises from **21.60%** under standard speculative decoding to **69.96%** with DIVERSED, while ROUGE-2 improves from **9.46** to **12.11**. On GSM8K, acceptance rises from **44.60%** to **72.61%** while accuracy remains at **67%**. On MBPP, acceptance increases from **26.30%** to **85.03%** with pass@1 staying at **53%**. Across settings, acceptance correlates strongly with end-to-end latency, so these gains translate into real wall-clock improvements rather than just nicer intermediate metrics.

A final lesson from the paper is that better draft-model task performance alone does not guarantee better speculative decoding. Fine-tuning the draft model can help in some cases, but it does not reliably improve acceptance. What matters most is **alignment between the draft and target conditional distributions**, and DIVERSED addresses that directly at verification time. For anyone working on efficient LLM inference, this suggests that the next gains may come less from better drafting alone and more from smarter verification.
